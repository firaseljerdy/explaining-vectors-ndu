SECTION 01

What Is an Autonomous Agent?

In Chapter 2, we learned about forces: gravity pulls objects down, friction slows them, springs pull them back. But in every case, the force was applied externally. The object didn't decide anything — it just obeyed Newton's second law.

An autonomous agent is different. It generates its own internal forces based on what it perceives. Craig Reynolds, in his foundational 1999 paper "Steering Behaviors for Autonomous Characters", defined three key properties:

  • Limited perception: The agent can only see part of its environment — not everything, just what's nearby or relevant.
  • Internal processing: It takes that perception and computes an action — a steering force — using simple rules.
  • No global leader: Each agent acts independently. There's no central controller telling everyone where to go.
My Takeaway

In Chapter 2, gravity and friction happened TO the object. In Chapter 5, the object DECIDES how to move. That's the shift from passive physics to active agency. The applyForce() pipeline is the same — the difference is who's computing the force.

Reynolds called these forces steering behaviors. The word "steering" is deliberate: like turning a car's steering wheel, you don't teleport to a new heading — you apply a corrective force that gradually changes your direction. The behaviors we'll cover (seek, arrive, flee, flow field following) are all variations of this one idea.

SECTION 02

The Vehicle Class

Our Vehicle builds directly on the Mover from Chapter 2. Same position, velocity, acceleration. Same applyForce() and update() methods. The only additions are two constraints that make it behave like an agent:

  • maxSpeed — limits how fast the vehicle can go. After adding acceleration to velocity, we clamp the velocity magnitude.
  • maxForce — limits how sharply it can steer. Every steering force is clamped to this maximum before being applied.
class Vehicle {
  constructor(x, y) {
    this.pos = new Vec(x, y);
    this.vel = new Vec(0, 0);
    this.acc = new Vec(0, 0);
    this.maxSpeed = 4;
    this.maxForce = 0.2;
  }

  applyForce(force) {
    this.acc.add(force);
  }

  update() {
    this.vel.add(this.acc);
    this.vel.limit(this.maxSpeed);
    this.pos.add(this.vel);
    this.acc.mult(0);  // reset each frame
  }
}
Two Knobs, Infinite Feels

maxSpeed is the gas pedal limit. maxForce is the steering wheel limit. A fast vehicle with low maxForce takes wide, sweeping turns — like a car on a highway. A slow vehicle with high maxForce is nimble and reactive — like a drone hovering. These two values control the entire personality of every behavior.

The vehicle is drawn as a triangle pointing in the direction of its velocity (using atan2(vel.y, vel.x) for the heading). This makes it visually obvious where the agent is headed and how it's turning.

SECTION 03

Steering Force = Desired − Velocity

This is the single most important formula in this chapter. Every behavior — seek, arrive, flee, follow — uses it.

steering = desired − velocity
The steering force is the difference between where you want to go and where you're currently going.

Think about driving a car. You're heading north at 60 km/h but want to go northeast. You don't teleport — you turn the wheel. The steering wheel applies a correction that gradually shifts your heading. Reynolds' insight was exactly this: don't set the velocity directly. Instead, compute the correction needed to go from your current velocity to your desired velocity.

The desired vector changes depending on the behavior:

  • Seek: desired points toward the target at full speed
  • Flee: desired points away from the target at full speed
  • Arrive: desired points toward the target, speed scaled by distance
  • Follow field: desired comes from the flow field cell lookup

But the subtraction desired − velocity is always the same. That's the elegance.

The Click Moment

This is Reynolds' genius: desired − velocity gives you the exact correction needed to go from where you ARE heading to where you WANT to head. Every behavior in this chapter just changes how you compute "desired." The rest of the math stays identical.

SECTION 04

Seek Behavior

Seek is the simplest steering behavior and the foundation for everything else. The algorithm has three steps:

  1. Calculate the desired velocity: a vector pointing from the vehicle's position to the target, normalized and scaled to maxSpeed.
  2. Compute the steering force: desired − velocity.
  3. Limit the steering force to maxForce.
seek(target) {
  let desired = Vec.sub(target, this.pos);
  desired.setMag(this.maxSpeed);

  let steer = Vec.sub(desired, this.vel);
  steer.limit(this.maxForce);
  return steer;
}

Notice that the desired velocity always has magnitude maxSpeed. The vehicle wants to go toward the target at full throttle. The limit(maxForce) call is what prevents instant turning — the vehicle can only adjust its course by a small amount per frame.

What Happens Visually

If maxForce is very high relative to maxSpeed, the vehicle turns on a dime — almost instant course corrections. If maxForce is low, the vehicle takes wide, arcing turns to reach the target. Try tweaking these values in the interactive slide to see the difference.

One consequence of seek: the vehicle never stops at the target. It arrives at full speed, overshoots, turns around, overshoots again, and ends up orbiting. To fix that, we need arrive.

SECTION 05

Arrive Behavior

Arrive solves seek's overshooting problem. The idea: define a slowing radius around the target. Outside the radius, behave exactly like seek (full speed). Inside, reduce the desired speed proportionally to the distance.

if (d < slowRadius): speed = maxSpeed × (d / slowRadius)
When inside the slowing radius, desired speed maps linearly from maxSpeed (at the edge) to 0 (at the target).
arrive(target, slowRadius) {
  let desired = Vec.sub(target, this.pos);
  let d = desired.mag();
  let speed = this.maxSpeed;

  if (d < slowRadius) {
    speed = map(d, 0, slowRadius, 0, this.maxSpeed);
  }

  desired.setMag(speed);
  let steer = Vec.sub(desired, this.vel);
  steer.limit(this.maxForce);
  return steer;
}

The only difference from seek is the if (d < slowRadius) check. Outside the radius, arrive IS seek. Inside, the map() function linearly scales the desired speed from maxSpeed at the edge down to 0 at the target itself.

Seek vs Arrive

Without arrive, a seeking vehicle perpetually overshoots its target and orbits around it — it always wants to go full speed, so it can never stop. Arrive solves this by reducing the desired speed as distance decreases. The vehicle decelerates smoothly and comes to rest exactly on the target.

Choosing the Slowing Radius

A large radius (200+ pixels) creates a gentle, gradual deceleration. A small radius (50 pixels) means the vehicle brakes hard at the last moment. The "right" value depends on the feel you want — experiment in the interactive slide to find a balance.

SECTION 06

Flee Behavior

Flee is the mirror image of seek. Instead of a desired velocity pointing toward the target, flee's desired velocity points away from it.

desired = normalize(position − target) × maxSpeed
Flee reverses the direction: position − target points away from the threat.

The subtraction is flipped: position − target instead of target − position. That's the only mathematical difference. The rest — normalize, scale to maxSpeed, subtract velocity, limit to maxForce — is identical.

flee(target, perceptionRadius) {
  let desired = Vec.sub(this.pos, target);
  let d = desired.mag();

  // Only flee if the threat is close enough
  if (d > perceptionRadius)
    return new Vec(0, 0);

  desired.setMag(this.maxSpeed);
  let steer = Vec.sub(desired, this.vel);
  steer.limit(this.maxForce);
  return steer;
}

The Perception Radius

We add a perception radius — the vehicle only flees when the threat is within range. If the threat is far away, the method returns a zero vector (no force). This makes the behavior more realistic: animals don't flee from predators that are kilometers away.

Flee vs Negative Seek

You could implement flee by negating the seek steering force: seek(target).mult(-1). This works mathematically, but computing the desired velocity directly (pointing away) is cleaner. It also makes adding the perception radius natural — you check distance before computing the force, not after.

SECTION 07

What Is a Flow Field?

A flow field (also called a vector field) divides the canvas into a grid of cells. Each cell stores a direction vector. When a vehicle enters a cell, it reads that cell's vector and treats it as its desired direction.

Think of it like invisible currents in a river. Each point in the water has a direction the current flows. A leaf floating on the surface follows whatever current it happens to be in.

class FlowField {
  constructor(resolution, w, h) {
    this.resolution = resolution;
    this.cols = Math.ceil(w / resolution);
    this.rows = Math.ceil(h / resolution);
    this.grid = [];  // 2D array of Vec
  }

  update(zOffset) {
    for (let r = 0; r < this.rows; r++) {
      this.grid[r] = [];
      for (let c = 0; c < this.cols; c++) {
        let angle = noise(c * 0.06, r * 0.06, zOffset);
        angle *= Math.PI * 4;
        this.grid[r][c] = new Vec(
          Math.cos(angle),
          Math.sin(angle)
        );
      }
    }
  }
}

Why Perlin Noise?

Perlin noise is a function that produces smooth, continuous random values. Unlike pure Math.random() (which is completely uncorrelated between calls), Perlin noise ensures that nearby inputs produce similar outputs. This means neighboring cells in our grid will point in similar directions, creating smooth, river-like flows instead of chaotic random directions.

Noise as a Design Tool

By mapping Perlin noise values to angles, we get organic-looking currents and eddies for free. The zOffset parameter lets us evolve the field over time — advancing it slowly each frame creates a living, breathing flow that vehicles navigate through.

The resolution controls cell size. A resolution of 20 means each cell is 20x20 pixels. Lower resolution = more cells = finer detail (but more computation). Higher resolution = fewer cells = broader strokes.

SECTION 08

Following a Flow Field

Once we have a flow field, following it is straightforward. Each frame, the vehicle:

  1. Determines which cell it's in by dividing its position by the resolution.
  2. Reads that cell's vector as the desired direction.
  3. Computes the steering force using the same desired − velocity formula.
follow(field) {
  let col = Math.floor(this.pos.x / field.resolution);
  let row = Math.floor(this.pos.y / field.resolution);

  // Clamp to grid bounds
  col = clamp(col, 0, field.cols - 1);
  row = clamp(row, 0, field.rows - 1);

  let desired = field.grid[row][col].copy();
  desired.setMag(this.maxSpeed);

  let steer = Vec.sub(desired, this.vel);
  steer.limit(this.maxForce);
  return steer;
}
Same Formula, Different Source

follow() is just seek() where the desired direction comes from a lookup table instead of a target point. The steering math — desired minus velocity, limited to maxForce — is identical. The only thing that changes across all these behaviors is how you compute the desired velocity.

One edge case to handle: when the vehicle is at the canvas edge (or wraps around), the cell lookup can go out of bounds. Always clamp the row and column indices to the grid dimensions before reading.

Wrapping at Edges

Flow field simulations typically wrap vehicles at the canvas edges: if a vehicle exits the right side, it reappears on the left. This creates the illusion of an infinite field and keeps all vehicles visible.

SECTION 09

Combining Behaviors

The real power of steering behaviors comes from combining them. A vehicle can seek food while simultaneously fleeing from a predator. How? The same way we combined forces in Chapter 2 — by calling applyForce() multiple times.

let seekForce = vehicle.seek(food);
let fleeForce = vehicle.flee(predator, 100);

// Weight the forces
seekForce.mult(1.0);   // normal priority
fleeForce.mult(2.0);   // survival is urgent

vehicle.applyForce(seekForce);
vehicle.applyForce(fleeForce);

Each behavior returns a steering force vector. You can weight these forces by multiplying them by a scalar before applying. A higher weight means that behavior dominates. In the example above, fleeing has twice the weight of seeking — survival beats hunger.

Tuning Weights

This is where it gets creative. By adjusting weights, you can make vehicles that are mostly curious (high seek weight) but cautious (moderate flee weight), or panicky (high flee, low seek). The same code, different numbers, completely different personality.

The accumulation pattern is identical to Chapter 2. Gravity + friction + spring force became seek + flee + flow field. The applyForce() function doesn't care where the force came from — it just adds them to the acceleration.

SECTION 10

Emergence from Simplicity

Here's the big picture: everything in this chapter follows one pattern.

  1. Compute a desired velocity (how you want to go).
  2. Subtract your current velocity (to get the correction).
  3. Limit the result to maxForce (you can only steer so fast).
  4. Apply it as a force.

Seek, arrive, flee, follow — they all use this formula. The only thing that changes is how you compute the desired velocity. That's the elegance of Reynolds' framework.

When you put many agents together, each independently running these simple rules, something remarkable happens: complex behavior emerges. Vehicles cluster, scatter, flow in streams, avoid obstacles — all without a single line of code telling them to do so. No central controller, no global plan.

The Foundation

Seek, arrive, flee, and flow field following are just the beginning. Reynolds also defined separation (don't crowd neighbors), alignment (steer toward the average heading of neighbors), and cohesion (steer toward the average position of neighbors). Combine all three and you get flocking — the famous "boids" algorithm that simulates bird flocks, fish schools, and crowd movement. All from the same desired − velocity formula.

That's Chapter 5. The whole thing boils down to: autonomous agents generate their own steering forces using simple local rules, and the same formula — desired minus velocity — drives every behavior. The force accumulation pattern from Chapter 2 carries forward: steering behaviors are just another set of forces to accumulate.
